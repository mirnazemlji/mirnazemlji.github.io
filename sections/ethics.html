Ethics in Research

As scientists we have one central duty: to respect the truth and never to distort it. As human beings working with other human beings — students and colleagues — we also have a clear responsibility to treat them justly. Here are some basic ethical principles that I believe every research mathematician and university professor should follow. The list is not exhaustive, nor is it limited to our profession.

Relations with Colleagues

Never discriminate. This is non-negotiable. Even if you have a low opinion of someone personally, you must treat them professionally and fairly in all mathematical interactions, including referee reports and hiring decisions. Apply the same standards to everyone.

Relations with Students

Show respect, give encouragement, and be fair. Students and society entrust you with their education. Do not betray that trust. Mark honestly, teach to the best of your knowledge, and accept criticism when it is justified. As a Ph.D. supervisor, give your best without counting credits. The thesis is the student’s work. Do not put your name on it just to increase your publication count. Genuine joint contributions are another matter and can be discussed.

Giving Credit

Give credit where it is due — to collaborators, to people whose ideas inspired you, and to AI tools when you use them. No exceptions. Do not rewrite the history of an idea, even if you improved it substantially. Tell it exactly as it happened.

Using Other People’s Work

Use only material that is intended to be public. This excludes research proposals, confidential drafts, ideas shared informally in conversation, or ongoing projects presented before publication. If you have a good method that could help someone else’s work, tell them so directly and offer assistance. Be satisfied with proper acknowledgment.

Proof Standards

Do not claim a proof when you know it is not one. An idea or a sketch is not a proof. Do not rely on your reputation to avoid careful checking. Read your own proofs. If you make a mistake, correct it publicly as soon as possible.

Respect for Mathematics

Never use mathematics to convey hidden messages, insinuations, personal attacks, or anything that is not mathematics. Do not drag the standards of the field down to serve your own purposes.
